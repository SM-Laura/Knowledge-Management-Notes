**How Knowledge Graphs Give Employee AI Systems The Data And Context They Need**
 This is a [Forrester](https://www.forrester.com/) Thought Leadership Paper on how knowledge graphs, like [Atlassian’s Teamwork Graph](https://www.atlassian.com/platform/teamwork-graph), can unlock AI’s full potential by connecting AI systems to unified, AI‑ready organizational knowledge.
 
**Organizations Seek AI-Driven Advantage But Struggle To Capture Meaningful Returns**
Despite large-scale investments in AI pilot programs, most organizations are not realizing the degree of business transformation or value originally projected. We found that: • Organizations invest in AI to improve business outcomes. Surveyed decision-makers said their companies’ top goals for employee AI adoption include reducing time spent on manual and repetitive tasks, improving decision-making through faster and more accurate insights, and enhancing customer experience. Together, these goals reflect the high expectations organizations have for AI to help transform their business outcomes. • Despite high expectations, AI investments are not delivering expected business value. More than half of decision-makers reported an inability to translate perceived productivity gains — or expected value — into tangible ROI (see Figure 1). In fact, most whose organizations have high AI adoption reported cost savings of less than 10%, and revenue gains of under 5%.1 AI strategy changes are needed to realize the value expected from AI investments.

**AI Systems Disconnected From Organizational Knowledge Are To Blame**

Survey respondents reported that the most common AI strategy challenges included difficulty integrating data across different tools and platforms and limited features (see Figure 2). These challenges are directly attributed to AI systems disconnected from organizational knowledge, which limit effectiveness. We found that: • Most AI systems can’t look across different applications, tools, and data sources (yet). Organizations primarily enable employees through embedded AI: 44% of decision-makers reported using AI agents and productivity features provided by vendors, while 43% develop AI agents using vendor-supplied tools. Consequently, the AI systems employees rely on are limited to the applications and data available within the vendor’s ecosystem. But work happens outside of a single tool and platform, so to accurately capture the knowledge required for employees to do their jobs, AI systems must be able to understand an organization’s entire tech stack, data environment, and knowledge base. • AI systems lack access to the organizational knowledge needed to empower employees. When implementing AI into their workflows, employees struggle to find the answers/outputs they want and find that AI systems lack contextual accuracy for their specific use cases. Put simply, AI models do not have the organizational knowledge needed to accurately answer questions. This frustration results in both low usage (i.e., employees don’t see value, so they avoid their organization’s AI tools) and productivity costs (i.e., employees are trying to use the AI tools but the inability to get the answers they are looking for leads to more work) (see Figure 3).

**ORGANIZATIONAL KNOWLEDGE IS NOT READY FOR AI** 
Improving AI access to organizational knowledge remains an unsolved problem for most. Organizations are unable to solve this problem because their knowledge is siloed and disconnected to begin with, making it unoptimized for AI use. We found that organizations are challenged in the following areas:
- **Maintenance and quality.** Rapid business transformation complicates knowledge. Over half of decision-makers cited the pace of organizational change as the top challenge to maintaining organizational knowledge (see Figure 4). Continuously evolving business models, organization structures, workflows, and teams make it nearly impossible to keep knowledge current. The result is inaccurate or out‑of-date knowledge, which means the information employees and AI tools rely on for process documentation, status, and even policy is often wrong.
- 78% of decision-makers admitted their organization has considerable or significant room for improving knowledge sharing.
![[Pasted image 20260326115513.png]]

- **Reachability**. There is not one single place where knowledge is stored. Less than half of decision-makers reported their organization’s knowledge is stored in any one specific database, knowledge base, or document management system. In fact, on average, they must go between five business tools to get the knowledge they need (see Figure 5). These discrete knowledge sources result in underutilized knowledge because employees (and even AI) don’t know it exists. 
- **Readability and quantity**. Knowledge remains largely unstructured and is missing critical elements. Organizations struggle with knowledge sources that are in incompatible formats. Many knowledge sources lack critical elements such as useful metadata (i.e., the operational data systems use to reach decisions and outcomes), lineage (i.e., lifecycle tracking including movement, transformations and interactions across systems/ users), and even proper governance. Forrester estimates that more than 65% of enterprise data lacks metadata.2 This makes it difficult for AI (and employees) to understand relevant knowledge.

**Connecting AI Systems To Organizational Knowledge Improves AI Usefulness**
The real value of AI in the enterprise is unlocked when it can access and reason over the full breadth of an organization’s knowledge — not just data siloed within individual applications. To make this possible, organizations need highquality, well-labeled, and semantically consistent knowledge. For example, concepts such as “order” or “revenue” use the same labels and structures across systems and datasets.3 We found that: 
- **Good organizational knowledge is critical for improving AI usefulness**. Surveyed decision-makers recognized that enhancing knowledge accessibility is important for ensuring AI quality. In fact, more than eight in 10 said their organization’s ability to improve AI outputs, scale across the enterprise, and use AI as a competitive advantage requires unifying knowledge and reducing data silos (see Figure 6). Achieving this requires systems that can access and reason over file shares, productivity tools, software development tooling, project management tools, and more. To ensure accuracy, recency, and fast retrieval, these systems must operate on a continuously updated data layer that captures new knowledge and evolving context in real time. 
- **Manual knowledge maintenance isn’t enough**. Organizations must fundamentally shift how they maintain knowledge. Knowledge can’t be a static repository that depends on humans doing their best. Keeping knowledge static leads to cherry-picked, manually curated, siloed information that can’t keep up with change. Knowledge bases need to function as living, comprehensive, accessible, and continuously learning systems. To make this a reality, automated systems are now being used to create and maintain the very knowledge bases that enterprise AI systems will rely on as key data layers. **WHERE AND HOW TO TRIGGER the automation?**
- **Connecting AI systems with organizational knowledge provides newfound business value**. Integrating organizational knowledge into AI models is a catalyst for unlocking the real value of AI. Surveyed decisionmakers indicated that their organizations would see the greatest benefits in improved customer experience, increased employee productivity, faster access to information across teams, and improved employee experience (see Figure 7).

**Knowledge Graphs Transform AI’s Ability To Reason Over All Organizational Knowledge**

The journey toward better data and organizational knowledge is not new. What’s different now is the opportunity to leverage AI on top of a knowledge graph-powered data platform. Knowledge graphs provide organizations with the foundation of knowledge that employees and systems need to more effectively do their jobs. AI systems need to understand relationships, team workflows, and organizational knowledge to answer employee questions (e.g., “What collaborators worked on a project with me,” or “What scoping document was the final one we sent to the client?”). Together, these enable smarter, more adaptive automation. We found that: 
- **Knowledge graphs improve accessibility for AI systems**. A knowledge graph enables a unified, AI-ready data layer to break down knowledge silos. This makes it possible to surface the connections, expertise, and context — which would otherwise remain hidden —needed to make AI useful. Expected benefits from implementing a knowledge graph included improved intra- and interplatform data unification, more informed employees, improved cross-team automation, and accelerated decision-making (see Figure 8).

**A knowledge graph is an interconnected knowledge base that uses flexible links (edges) between entities (nodes) to define relationships. For example, an “employee” could be linked to projects, role, communication tools, and more. The graph would enable a search for all of communications related to a given project and its associated developers without requiring this information to be stored in a single location.**

- **Despite the ability to transform AI effectiveness, knowledge graphs remain a black hole for many organizations**. Before defining a knowledge graph in our survey, we asked decision-makers to indicate how familiar they were with the concept. Just 34% of decision-makers indicated they have an in-depth understanding of what knowledge graphs are and how they can be used. Even though knowledge graphs are not a new technology, many organizations lack a full understanding of their capabilities and benefits, especially the value and increased AI effectiveness they can help drive by supplying AI agents with missing contextual data


**EMBEDDED KNOWLEDGE GRAPH-POWERED PRODUCTS PROVIDE THE FOUNDATION NEEDED FOR ENTERPRISE AI SYSTEMS** 
Nine in 10 decision-makers reported their organization would be interested or very interested in knowledge graph-powered products for the ability to use AI for more robust applications and use cases (see Figure 9). Augmenting AI with an AI-embedded knowledge graph-powered product, such as enterprise knowledge and collaboration system, offers a fast, easy way for organizations to improve knowledge accessibility while avoiding the challenges of building and maintaining their own knowledge graphs. The result is amplified value from both existing data stewardship investments and platforms that already rely on knowledge graphs as a foundational structure. We found that: 
- **Partnering with strategic tech providers top adoption plans**. Despite interest in the technology, most organizations lack required skill sets to make and maintain graphs capable of representing the breadth of enterprise knowledge. More than half (51%) of decision-makers indicated partnering with a strategic tech provider was their top priority for improving organizational knowledge (see Figure 10). 
- **Using embedded knowledge graph-powered products from strategic tech providers address the challenges of building from scratch**. Building a knowledge graph from scratch is often resource intensive. Using a partner allows organizations to leverage an existing ontological map and simplify data hygiene. Instead of having to agree on the definition of a specific phrase to utilize the data (e.g., is “works with” defined based on reporting line or actual project collaboration?), the knowledge graph can simply be consumed without the accompanying philosophical debate. Using a tech provider with a knowledge graph embedded into an AI data platform creates a more effective enterprise knowledge and search system. This helps organizations decrease technical debt and benefit from economies of scale.5

**Key Recommendations** 
Investing in knowledge-graph powered technology solutions presents a significant — and fast-growing — opportunity to harness the true power of enterprise AI for work. Forrester’s in-depth survey of 275 AI decision-makers yielded several important recommendations: 
**Embrace AI data platforms underpinned by knowledge graphs to ground AI in your business context.** 
The most important step you can take in improving AI relevancy is simply providing AI tools with an effective data index. AI-ready data platforms are growing in importance, and knowledge graphs provide their essential cypher layer to connect data, relationships, and business context. In addition to making it easier for employees to get their questions answered (like searching “open tasks for Acme”), knowledge graphs provide the same benefit to AI tools. Prioritizing products that have AI-ready data platforms with knowledge graphs as a foundational data layer (or compatibility with existing graphs) provides a critical data foundation. 
**Invest in cross-ecosystem graphs.** 
Organizational knowledge (and work) is involuntarily decentralized today. For any data layer to serve as a foundation for context-aware AI systems, it must be able to seamlessly connect insights and information from all tools that employees use in their day-today (for example, with Acme open tasks, looking into project management, collaborative work management [CWM], CRM and even email tools). 
Invest in tool ecosystems that graphs can effectively bridge across multiple vendor ecosystems. Once online, ensure you are enabling the required connections to the graph. Vendors who can serve as this trusted partner and who can assist with this ongoing integration must be prioritized. 
**Invest in making knowledge management a shared (and easy) responsibility**. While knowledge graphs should provide the key connective tissue for organizational knowledge, it pays to make sure that what its connecting is right in the first place. Most organizations today suffer from incomplete, disorganized, and out-of-date formal knowledge. Part of this will be solved by graphs, and part of it needs humans in the loop to help fix (for example, answering, “What is our strategic priority with the Acme account?”). 
Two changes are required: 
1) **Build a knowledge management practice to evaluate both where information lives and the state of it**, and 
2) **use this practice to help your organization change knowledge management from a silo to a shared sport**. For example, building collaborative knowledge review into not just a valued practice but also embedding responsibilities into team goals.
