### What is an infographic?

What is an infographic? It's a visual representation of information, data or knowledge. It presents the material quickly and clearly and more efficiently than explaining concepts in text. A well-designed infographic increases the willingness on the part of the viewer to engage, simply because our brains can absorb the information quicker. Infographics allow us to see patterns and trends. ### A brief history of infographics
While the popularity of infographics is a relatively recent phenomenon, infographics have been around for a long time. Let's take a look at some landmarks in the history of infographics. In 1857, English nurse Florence Nightingale used infographics to lobby for better conditions in military hospitals. She used a Coxcomb chart, a combination of stacked, bar, and pie charts to depict the number and causes of deaths during each month of the Crimean War. In 1861, French civil engineer Charles Joseph Minard created an infographic depicting Napoleon's disastrous march on Moscow in 1812. He showed changing variables that contributed to Napoleon's failure: the army's direction as they traveled, the locations that the troops passed through, the size of the army as troops died from hunger and wounds, and the freezing temperatures they experienced. In 1900, civil rights campaigner W.E.B. Du Bois created a series of infographics to humanize the African American experience. Between 1925 and 1934, the Social and Economic Museum of Vienna developed the Isotype, or International System of Typographic Picture Education. Otto Neurath was the museum's founding director. Gerd Arntz was the artist responsible for creating the graphics. The aim of the Isotypes was to represent social facts pictorially and to bring statistics to life by making them visually attractive and memorable. One of the museum's catchphrases was, "To remember simplified pictures "is better than to forget accurate figures." An important principle of Isotype is that greater quantities are represented by a greater number of the same size pictogram rather than by an enlarged pictogram. In Neurath's view, variation in size does not allow accurate comparison, whereas repeated pictograms can be counted, if necessary. In 1933, Harry Beck created a map for the London Underground, an updated version of which is still in use today. ### What makes a good infographic?
A good infographic should meet most if not all of the following criteria. The elements of the infographic, illustrations, symbols, data visualizations, and the supporting text should tell a cohesive story. It should take a large amount of information and make it easier to understand than text. It should provide a new angle, a new way of seeing familiar information. The infographic should present the information in a way that is both logically and visually organized with hierarchy and navigational cues. Of course, it should be visually appealing. It should make good use of type and color. Because the goal of an infographic is to communicate information as efficiently as possible, if it contains multiple messages, the viewer may be confused. This beautiful visual history of every manned Apollo flight displays a wealth of information, but stays focused on its key message and doesn't get sidetracked into other areas of the history of space exploration. The information should be handled responsibly, without misleading the viewer. The infographic should use reputable sources of data and include links to those sources. And of course, it should be concise. The text should be honed into bite-size pieces. The text should support the images and not the other way around.

### Types of infographic

- Timelines = cronology/evolution
- How to = instructional/process driven
- Informational = illustrated lists
- Side by side comparisons
- Statistical = use graphs and charts to show info
- Maps =anchor info to a location
- Visualised numbers = highlight figures with scales and typography
- Hierarchical = emphasize the hierarchical nature of information

### The importance of planning

Here are some key questions to ask yourself when designing an infographic. Who is the audience? Are they academic or informal? Young or old? What are their interests? What is the purpose of the infographic? Is it to retain the audience attention while they are reading an article? Or, establish your expertise in a field? Or, to sell a product? Or, to lobby for positive change? Or, is it to increase brand awareness? Define the medium. Is it a print graphic at a fixed size or is it for the web, where it can be continuously scrolling? Start out with a sketch and then make yourself a wireframe that blocks out the areas of your page or screen with the different sections of the infographic.

### Use icons

Wherever possible, replace text in your infographic with icons. These icons should be in a consistent style. Here are some icon resources. FontAwesome is an incredibly useful picture font comprised of many useful icons including social media icons. You can combine it with the Font Awesome Cheatsheet to help you find the icon that you're after. Then, copy and paste it into Illustrator. The noun project is a crowd-sourced repository of vector icons. You can download these icons in SVG format. Photoshop has the, often overlooked but useful, custom shape tool. In the 2020 version of Photoshop, the number of custom shapes has been expanded. Once drawn, the custom shape can be copied and pasted into Illustrator as a compound shape which is completely scalable. In addition, a browser search for Photoshop custom shapes will return many results of sites from where you can download custom shapes. As well as using these and other icon resources, you may want to create your own icons.
